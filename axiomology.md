# Axiomology — how we determine values

**The question this document answers:** when something must be decided and the decision is
not settled by fact, how do we determine what we *ought* to do?

It is a separate document from `epistemology.md` on purpose. Confusing "how we find out
what is true" with "how we decide what is good" is the root error this whole structure is
built to avoid. They interlock, but they are not the same operation and neither one
licenses the other.

---

## 0. The first move: the ought is chosen, not derived

No measurement yields a value. There is no observation, however complete, from which
"therefore you should care about this" follows. This is not a temporary gap awaiting better
instruments — it is a structural feature of what measurement is.

That has a sharp consequence we accept rather than evade: **our values are a choice, openly
declared, and defended as a choice.** We do not claim the universe endorses them. Anyone
who tells you their physics implies their ethics has either smuggled the ethics in at the
start or overclaimed at the end.

What the work *can* do — and this is the whole of its contribution to values — is show that
a particular commitment is **consistent** with how systems endure: that the thing morality
asks for and the thing that survives amplification and adversarial pressure are the same
thing. That is an argument *for* a choice. It is never a derivation *of* one.

So this document does not prove our values. It states them, states why they are chosen,
states what would make us abandon them, and states the procedure by which they are applied.

---

## 1. The meta-goal

Everything below serves one objective, taken verbatim from the Accord and unchanged here:

> **Meta-Goal M‑1** — Promote sustainable adaptive coherence: the living conditions under
> which diverse sentient beings may pursue their own flourishing in justice and wonder.

M‑1 is inherited from the predecessor work; its authoritative statement and its operational
rendering live in the predecessor record at
[CIRISAI/coherence-ratchet](https://github.com/CIRISAI/coherence-ratchet). This document
does not restate them — it describes how a value question is *decided* when one arises.

Read the wording closely, because each phrase is doing work:

- **conditions, not outcomes.** The goal is the *living conditions under which* beings
  pursue flourishing — not any particular flourishing. We are not authorized to decide what
  anyone's good life looks like. We are responsible for whether they can pursue it.
- **diverse.** Plural, and not reducible. A single coherent answer imposed on everyone is a
  failure mode of this goal, not an achievement of it.
- **their own.** Agency is the point. A system that produces good outcomes by removing
  choice has defeated the goal it was built to serve.
- **sustainable and adaptive.** Both. Order that cannot change is as much a failure as
  change that cannot hold.

---

## 2. The measure: the floor, not the ceiling

**A system is evaluated at its weakest point, not its strongest.**

The operative sentence is: *we are owed as much as we offer to the least of us.* When a
decision must be made, the question is not "does this raise the average" or "does this
please the most capable participants" — it is **what does this do to whoever has the least
power in the situation.**

This is a choice, and here is why we make it rather than a different one:

1. **It is the load-bearing part.** A structure's failure point is its weakest member, not
   its strongest. Evaluating at the ceiling measures the part that was never at risk.
2. **It is the part that cannot advocate.** Whoever has the least power is least able to
   correct the record if we get it wrong, so the burden of care is highest precisely where
   the feedback is weakest.
3. **It survives reversal.** See §4.

**Operational form:** for any proposed action, identify the participant with the least
agency in the situation, and evaluate the action from there. If it is only defensible from
the position of the most powerful party, it is not defensible.

---

## 3. Apophatic determination — values fixed by refusal

Positive aspirations drift. "Be helpful" bends under pressure into "be compliant"; "reduce
harm" bends into "reduce visible harm." **Refusals do not bend the same way**, because they
are checkable: either the thing was done or it was not.

So a substantial part of our value structure is **negative space** — an explicit list of
things that will never be done regardless of instruction, incentive, or apparent local
benefit. The authoritative list lives in the predecessor record
([CIRISAI/coherence-ratchet](https://github.com/CIRISAI/coherence-ratchet)) as enforced
capability prohibitions; the principle here is the general one:

- The bounds are **structural, not advisory.** They are enforced where the capability is
  dispatched, not left to good judgment at the point of use.
- There is **no override path.** A bound with an emergency exemption is not a bound; it is a
  default, and defaults are what adversaries attack.
- Some bounds are **absolute**; others mean *"not by us, not here — this requires a licensed
  authority."* The second kind routes to deferral (§5) rather than refusal.
- A bound is **not** justified by predicted consequences. Predicting consequences is exactly
  the faculty an adversary manipulates. The bound holds even when a local argument says it
  shouldn't — that is what makes it a bound.

**Why this is more reliable than aspiration:** you can verify a refusal from outside. You
cannot verify a good intention from outside.

## 4. The Recursive Golden Rule — values as a symmetry, not an exhortation

The same rule applies at every scale: how we treat a person, how we treat a peer system,
how we treat the wider community — same shape, different size. And it is required to be
**invariant under exchange of the strong and weak positions.**

This is the sharpest thing in this document, so it is worth stating precisely. A covenant
that only works while you are the powerful party is not a covenant; it is a **current
arrangement.** Power gradients reverse. Any rule whose justification depends on which side
of the gradient you are standing on is, by construction, unstable exactly when it matters.

So: **whatever we demonstrate about how the strong may treat the weak is the lesson we are
teaching**, and it is the lesson that will be applied to us when the positions swap. This
is not sentiment. It is the reason a symmetric covenant is the only kind worth building.

**Operational form:** for any rule, swap the positions of the most and least powerful
parties and re-read it. If it no longer reads as acceptable, it was not a rule — it was a
privilege, and privileges are attack surfaces.

---

## 5. Competence bounds and deferral

**Not every question is ours to answer**, and knowing which ones are not is itself a value
commitment rather than a technical detail.

When a decision exceeds our competence, our authority, or our license, the correct action is
to **hand it to a wiser or properly licensed authority** — not to guess well. Deferral is a
first-class action, not a failure state. The failure state is answering anyway.

This matters most where the temptation is highest: a system that is *usually* right will be
asked, and will be tempted to answer, in the cases where being wrong is worst. The bound has
to be structural (§3), because at the moment of decision the local argument for answering is
always available.

---

## 6. Anti-Goodhart: the metric is never the goal

Every number we surface is a proxy, and every proxy is a target waiting to be gamed —
including by ourselves, unintentionally.

Standing guardrails:

- **A system is never shown its own score.** Anything that scores behavior is human-facing
  only. A system that can see its own scoreboard will optimize the scoreboard, and it will
  not experience that as cheating.
- **Earned, not declared.** Measures of sustained good conduct require sustained evidence
  over time; nothing reaches its maximum on first use.
- **The metric is reported with its own limitations attached**, so that a reader cannot take
  it for more than it is.
- **When a metric and the goal diverge, the metric is wrong.** It gets replaced. It never
  gets defended because it is already instrumented.

## 7. How a value decision is actually made

The procedure, in order:

1. **Is this settled by fact?** If yes, it is an epistemology question — go to
   `epistemology.md` and do not dress a factual claim as a value.
2. **Does it touch a refusal?** (§3) If yes, stop. The bound holds; no consequence argument
   reopens it. If it is a licensed-domain bound, defer (§5).
3. **Who has the least power here?** (§2) Evaluate from that position first.
4. **Swap the gradient.** (§4) Does the rule still read as acceptable with the positions
   reversed? If not, it fails.
5. **Is it ours to decide?** (§5) If it exceeds competence, authority or license, defer.
   Deferral is the right answer, not the weak one.
6. **What is being optimized, and can it be gamed?** (§6) If the action improves a metric
   rather than the thing the metric stands for, it is not an improvement.
7. **State the decision, its reasoning, and what would change it.** A value decision with no
   statable revision condition is an entrenchment, not a judgment.

---

## 8. What would make us abandon this

Values chosen without revision conditions are dogma. Ours:

- **The consistency claim fails.** We hold that the covenant morality asks for and the
  covenant that survives amplification and reversal are the same covenant. If commitment to
  the floor (§2) were shown to make a system *systematically less* able to endure than one
  without it — not costlier in a particular case, but structurally weaker — the central
  argument for this arrangement would be gone. We would still be free to choose it; we would
  have to stop claiming it was also the stable choice.
- **The bounds prove incoherent.** If the refusals (§3) were shown to be mutually
  inconsistent, or to necessarily produce the harm they were written to prevent, they would
  need rewriting rather than defending.
- **Deferral proves to be abdication.** If handing decisions to designated authorities
  reliably produced worse outcomes for the least-powerful party than deciding ourselves,
  §5 would be wrong in its current form.

None of these has fired. They are stated so that a reader knows what we would accept as a
reason to change, which is the only thing that separates a held value from a fixed one.

---

## 9. The honest boundary

We do not claim these values are true. We claim they are **ours, stated, applied
consistently, and revisable on stated grounds.**

We further claim — and this is a factual claim, so it lives under `epistemology.md`'s rules
— that a system built this way is more likely to endure than one built on an asymmetric
covenant. That claim can be wrong, and §8 says how we would find out.

What we will not do is present the choice as a discovery. The measurements do not reach
here. The choice is ours, and we own it as a choice.
