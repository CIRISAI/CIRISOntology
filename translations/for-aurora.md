## Gravity can weigh every single thing, but it cannot read even one story.

*measured — real people measured it and kept the notes*

There is one checker in the whole world that nothing can say no to. Its name is gravity. Warm things count. Even light, like sunshine, counts. Gravity feels all of it. Nothing hides. Everything is on gravity's list.

So the one checker that checks everything still misses a whole big part: the way things are put together, what they mean, whether they are honest. That missed part has a name, like a new friend: the Logos.

**What would make us surer:** This is already measured about as tightly as tight can be. Each time someone measures even more carefully and it still holds, it gets a little surer.

## Why prizes get gamed: a single number is a two-at-a-time peek, and the cheating hides in the whole.

And what a computer proved earlier is narrower than it sounds: a two-at-a-time summary CAN read zero on parts that are completely tied together, and no summary can hand back what it threw away. That was proved about coins, not about companies. Carrying it over to people and prizes is the bet.

**What would prove this wrong:** We say only that this lopsidedness is ONE source of time's arrow, so the test is aimed there. Build or find a place where togetherness is hard to make and easy to lose, exactly as described, and where time has no direction at all — or runs backward. One such place, and the lopsidedness is not doing the work we said.

**What would make us surer:** Find a place where you can turn the lopsidedness up and down, and watch time's arrow get weaker as you take the lopsidedness away.

## Our world is the only kind of world where thinking bodies and real choosing can both live.

*a bet we choose to make — not evidence*

Now put all the pieces together, like blocks.

For a pick to be really yours, there has to be a spot in the world that no checking can reach. Think why. If every way things sit had a tag, and every tag could be read, then every pick you made would already be a line in someone's book before you even made it. That would not be your pick at all.

But there is a second need pulling the other way. For a mind to be made of ordinary stuff — like you are — the world's habits have to be real and safe to trust. Blocks must fall the same way every time. And that means the world really must keep a book. It must remember how things go.

Our world has exactly this shape, and only this shape does both jobs at once. It keeps the book: the world remembers. It has a weigher, gravity, that feels how heavy every bag is but never opens a bag. And it has a hidden room where shared meaning lives, that no two-at-a-time peeking can get into — the Logos.

So here is our bet: that hidden, no-peeking room is not a broken part of the world. It IS the plan. It is the one shape where beings built out of habits can also be free.

How sure are we? A bet we choose. Not evidence, and not proof. We say plainly what would make us give it up.

**What would prove this wrong:** Either find alone is enough. One: nature turns out to have a checker that CAN read how things are arranged — then there is no hiding room, and no room left for real choosing. Two: someone builds a little toy world that keeps NO book at all about how its parts go together, and its habits are still safe to trust — then a world does not need this shape after all.

**What would make us surer:** This one may stay a bet, because it says what MUST be, not what is. It gets surer only as the bets under it — being-awake, and meaning living in the whole — each earn "measured" on their own.

## Maybe the laws of the world are simply its oldest habits.

*a bet we choose to make — and the furthest-out bet on the whole page*

A wise man named Peirce made a strange suggestion long ago. What if the laws of nature are not eternal rules that existed before everything, but HABITS the world fell into — regular ways of going that took hold early and have simply held ever since?

Nobody could do much with the idea, because there was no way to count habits. We think there may now be one: the world's coin-book. In that language, a law of the world would be the oldest line in the book — an arrangement so old, and so completely paid up, that we mistake it for the paper itself. We have not actually counted one. We are only saying WHERE the counting could happen.

We once leaned on a hint here, and it has since died, so we take it back in the open. The hint was that the number π (say "pie") being stamped all over physics might be a readable fingerprint of habit. That hint is now dead. π's own careful count — the test that killed the pi bet on this page — sorted every place π shows up and found each one to be either a way of measuring turning (like counting a whole circle as 2 π) or forced by round, smooth shapes. No leftover anywhere. So π's everywhere-ness is grammar too, not history. There is no π-fingerprint to read, and we say so plainly instead of quietly dropping it.

That leaves this bet standing on its own test alone, and weaker for it. The only handle left is whether the evenness of space and the repeating our world runs on could have been different — and we have no tool that can check that yet. We mark the claim down honestly.

How sure are we? This is the furthest-out bet on the page, and we mark it as such. The idea is Peirce's, not ours. What we add is a way it could be counted.

**What would prove this wrong:** Someone shows the evenness of space and the repeating our world runs on had to be that way — that they follow from something more basic and could not have settled otherwise. Then they are grammar, not habit, and the bet loses. (Careful: nobody managing to derive them is NOT a point for us. An unexplained leftover never counts as a yes here.)

**What would make us surer:** The π-fingerprint handle is gone — we ran it, and it came up empty. With no tool yet for whether the world's rules could have been different, this stays the furthest from sure on the whole page.

# Bets about the dark sky

Most of the world gives off no light. We can only feel it by its pull. These are our bets about that dark part — and two of them are RIVALS that fight each other.

## Dark energy is the sky's balance of togetherness — read the careful way, it can only shrink, never grow.

*a bet we choose to make — not evidence*

Look at the night sky. Most of what is out there does not shine. One kind of that dark stuff is called dark energy — the push that makes everything spread apart faster and faster.

Think of a coin-book again, a running balance of all the togetherness across the whole sky. This careful bet reads that balance the strict way: per unit, evened out for size, so it counts pattern and not amount — the same rule the rest of this page lives by.

Read that way, the balance has a special property, one we can argue with plain math: it can only go DOWN over time, or hold still. It can never go up. So this dark energy can fade toward barely-there, but it can never get stronger. Grown-ups call the forbidden get-stronger kind "phantom." This reading says: no phantom, ever.

And because it is evened-out, ordinary clumping does not move it at all — we measured that, to fifteen decimal places, as close to zero as anyone can figure. So this dark energy is perfectly smooth. It never pools in one place. (One careful word: what stays smooth is REAL lumpiness a telescope could catch; a tiny bookkeeping wrinkle from just picking when "now" is does not count. And this holds where clumping can be measured, not at the very biggest sizes of all, where "smooth" turns into a tricky word.)

Now the hard part, out loud: this careful bet BETS AGAINST our own other bet, the next one, which says dark energy WAS the runaway kind long ago. One test — a sky team called DESI, and its third big book — points the two opposite ways. At most one can win, and a middling answer could take both. And today's DESI numbers lean toward the runaway past, which is a point AGAINST this careful bet — it sits a good way off from what the sky shows, about as far off as plain unchanging dark energy. One hope: much of that lean rides on how bright faraway exploding stars are, and if those brightness numbers hide a mistake, this bet and plain dark energy climb back together. That is why DESI's shape-only check, done without the exploding stars, is the real decider.

How sure are we? A bet we choose. The one piece that stands on math is narrow: the evened-out balance can only shrink. Reading dark energy AS that balance is the bet, and it is ours to lose.

**What would prove this wrong:** DESI's third book, checked the frozen way and from the sky's SHAPE alone, shows dark energy was ever the runaway "phantom" kind in the past, clearly — the math says that can never happen. OR: dark energy is ever caught clumping, at any size — a shrinking evened-out balance cannot clump.

**What would make us surer:** DESI's third book comes back with NO runaway past and no clumping — that match moves this toward measured and wins the contest with the next bet. Re-checking the "can only shrink" math in a computer here would give it a checked spine too.

## Dark energy is the same balance — read as a grand total that grew, then crested.

*a bet we choose to make — and the shakier of the two, we say so*

This is the same idea as the one before it, read a different way — and the two are rivals, not partners.

Again, dark energy is the running balance of togetherness. But this bet adds it up as a GRAND TOTAL, not per-unit — and the number of things being counted grows over time. As the sky makes galaxies and clumps, there are more and more pieces to add in. So the total climbs while pieces keep forming, reaches a high point when forming slows, and eases down after.

A total that climbs and then eases has a special moment: the crest. Before the crest the balance was growing — the runaway kind. At the crest it holds steady. After, it fades. So this bet says dark energy CROSSED the key line once, long ago, at a definite moment we can point to.

Now the honesty part, and it is heavy, because our own record says it. We call this bet a look-back, not a clean guess: the choice to add up the grand total, instead of the strict per-unit way, was made AFTER we saw the grand total happened to land near what the sky shows. It goes against our own stated rule, which prefers per-unit.

Worse, this total is built from a CLUMPY thing — the count of galaxies in a place, which is higher where stuff is denser. A dark energy built from a clumpy count is at risk of clumping too. And here is a hard result we worked out: for any ORDINARY dark energy — the kind that acts like a normal stuff spread through space — being exactly smooth while ALSO changing in strength is not possible; it would need its ripples to go faster than light, which nature does not allow. So a normal-stuff version of this bet MUST clump a little at the biggest sizes. The only way out is to make the grand total not a stuff at all, but one whole-sky number tied to a single special way of slicing time — an allowed but heavy kind of theory that we have not built. So the smoothing is not a footnote to add later: it is either impossible or expensive, and the giant-empty-place measurements will help decide.

Today the sky leans this bet's way — it sits closer to what DESI shows than its careful twin does, because DESI currently leans toward a crossing. Hold that lightly, though — the lean may be a brightness-of-stars mistake, and DESI's shape-only check decides.

How sure are we? A bet, and the shakier of the two, by our own count.

**What would prove this wrong:** DESI's third book, checked the frozen way, must put the crossing moment inside the little spot we drew ahead of time. Outside it — including no crossing at all — and this bet is dead. OR: dark energy is caught pooling in dense places, or the light-cooling from the giant empty places comes in at the strong clumpy level instead of the mild smooth one.

**What would make us surer:** Two things together: DESI's crossing lands inside the little spot we drew, AND the extra machinery that keeps the grand total smooth is really built, not just hoped for.

## The sky looks a little smoother today than the baby-picture predicts — and a smooth, fading dark energy pushes exactly that way.

*a bet we choose to make — leans on the careful dark-energy bet*

Here is a small real puzzle. Take the baby-picture of the universe (a very old light, from when everything was tiny and hot) and use it to guess how clumpy the sky should be today. Then measure today's clumpiness with bending light. For a while, the measured clumpiness came out a little LOWER than the guess.

Our reading connects this to the careful balance bet. If dark energy is that smooth, fading balance, two things follow, and we checked the direction of both with our own tool. First, the balance never clumps — it adds no lumps of its own. Second, because it slowly fades instead of holding perfectly steady, there was a touch MORE of it long ago, which puts a little extra brake on how clumps grow. Both push the same way: today ends up a little smoother.

Be exact about how much this buys. The push is the right DIRECTION but SMALL — well under how big the puzzle is — and a small push does not explain a big puzzle. We are not claiming to fix it. We are saying the direction of our push matches the direction of the puzzle, which is a thing that could have come out backward and did not.

One more honest turn: the best newest sky-look (a survey called KiDS-Legacy) now finds today's clumpiness almost exactly where the baby-picture said — the puzzle has mostly faded. So there may be less here to explain than there once looked to be. We say so.

How sure are we? A bet. It leans entirely on the careful dark-energy bet above — it is a matching-check on that bet, not new proof — and only its DIRECTION is firm, not its size.

**What would prove this wrong:** Better clumpiness measures show today's sky is actually MORE clumpy than the baby-picture guess, not less — then our push points the wrong way. Also, if the careful dark-energy bet dies, this one dies with it.

**What would make us surer:** A planned-first check where the careful dark-energy push predicts the exact smoothness gap, on new sky-clues — turning a matching direction into a matching number.

## The old "too-cold giant empty places" signal, if it is real, is the grand-total balance clumping — the same fight, on a second field.

*a bet we choose to make — leans on the grand-total dark-energy bet*

When old baby-picture light crosses a giant EMPTY place in the sky — a supervoid — on its way to us, it cools by a tiny bit. For over ten years, some sky-looks have found this cooling a few times STRONGER than plain dark energy predicts. Other, bigger sky-looks find no extra at all. It is a real, unsettled argument among grown-ups.

Here is our reading, and it is the OTHER bet's side. The grand-total balance is built from a clumpy thing — the count of galaxies — so it clumps a little, pooling where stuff pools. Dark energy pooling in and around empty places makes their light-cooling STRONGER. So the grand-total bet actually WANTS this extra cooling to be real: the too-cold empty places would be its fingerprint.

Now the fight, plainly, because it is the same fight on a second field. The careful (per-unit) bet is perfectly smooth and says NO extra — so it needs these puzzling measurements to turn out to be mistakes that wash away with better looking. The grand-total bet is FED by the extra — it needs the signal to survive. Both cannot win. One planned program of measuring these empty places, with DESI and later a survey called Euclid, will settle it, and it points the two bets opposite ways.

Honest state of things: today the measurements disagree with each other, and a few-times-stronger signal sits right in the arguing middle — not clearly yes, not clearly no. This is a live tug-of-war, not a result.

How sure are we? A bet. It leans on the grand-total balance bet, and lives or dies on the same evidence, from a second direction.

**What would prove this wrong:** The planned-first empty-place test, rules fixed before looking, comes back with cooling that matches plain dark energy — no extra — clearly. Then this bet dies, and the careful bet is helped. (And if the grand-total bet dies for another reason, this dies with it.)

**What would make us surer:** The planned-first test returns extra cooling, clearly above plain dark energy, seen again and again across different lists of empty places — moving this toward measured and the fight toward the grand-total side.

## If dark energy is the running total of the sky's togetherness, it could do nothing before there was any togetherness — so it cannot be the fix for the "too-fast" puzzle.

*a bet we choose to make — and the only one here fed by no shared data*

There is a second famous crack in the sky-story, the "too-fast" puzzle: the universe seems to be spreading a bit faster today than the baby-picture predicts. One popular patch is called early dark energy — a burst of extra dark energy switched on for a moment way back near the baby-picture time, long before any stars or galaxies.

Our whole idea forbids that patch, and this is a real prediction, not a wish. On our reading, dark energy is the running total of TOGETHERNESS — of things working in step. Before there are things to work in step, the total is basically nothing. A balance that is the bookkeeping of galaxies cannot be big in a time with no galaxies. So there is no room, in our idea, for an early burst.

This bet has real skin in the game, and it points AGAINST a patch many grown-ups would like to work. It is also all on its own: it is not fed by the same sky-clues as our other dark bets, so if it survives it is real outside help, and if it fails it hurts the whole dark-balance idea, both rivals at once.

Where things stand today: early dark energy is NOT found. The careful studies say it is unlikely and does not really fix the puzzle, with any early burst held to at most a few small parts of everything. One telescope has shown a mild lean toward it that the others do not back up. So the bet is alive, and a real measurement could still knock it over.

How sure are we? A bet — a clean result of our idea, said so it can be knocked down.

**What would prove this wrong:** A found early-dark-energy burst — a real several-parts-in-a-hundred of everything, back near the baby-picture time — seen clearly and agreed across the big telescopes, not just liked by one. That would show the balance was big before there was togetherness, which our idea says is impossible.

**What would make us surer:** It does not get surer by early dark energy staying missing — a missing thing is not a yes. It earns surer only if we can work out, from the count idea, an exact ceiling on how much dark energy there could be before stars, that the sky then meets or beats.

## Maybe dark matter is the paper the world's stories are written on.

*a bet we choose to make — the weaker half of our dark bet, and we say so*

You know how a book has paper? The story sits on the paper; the paper holds the story but adds no words of its own.

Grown-ups talk about something in space, all around, that no one can see. They call it dark matter. Our guess: the dark stuff is really there, like the paper in a book — the world's stories written on it — and it is very, very quiet. In our guess it does only a few things: space slowly gets bigger, and it spreads thinner, like one pat of butter on a bigger and bigger bread; if two bits meet, they slide right through like ghosts; and it pulls, the way all heavy things pull. But that is everything. Nothing else.

Now the honest part. We did not catch it. We saw no new clue about what it is. We only read old clues a new way. And this guess does not stand on its own — whatever help it has, it borrows from our dark-energy bet. In fact that bet has split into two rivals now — a careful one that forbids any runaway past, and a grand-total one that predicts a crossing — and one test, DESI, will cut between them. This paper-and-ink guess borrows its help from whichever of the two survives. If both fall, this one is left standing on nothing but its own test, and should be read that way. Other people read the same clues and tell a different story, where the dark stuff moves and does things by itself.

How sure are we? Not sure. A bet we choose, like picking a team before the game. Picking a team proves nothing. We promise to say exactly what would make us give it up.

**What would prove this wrong:** The dark stuff is ever caught, for sure, doing something that is not just quiet pulling and slow thinning — bumping, wiggling, shoving, anything of its own. Or a fair test, rules written first, shows the "it moves by itself" story fits the same clues BETTER than ours — not just as well, better.

**What would make us surer:** A planned-first head-to-head, won: the quiet-paper reading fitting the same clues strictly better than the "it moves by itself" readings.

# The humble part: what looking can never do, and what we do not know yet

## Looking can never tell us: did someone mean to make the tidy thing, or did it just happen all by itself?

*a bet we choose to make — not evidence*

Pretend you find your blocks stacked in a tall, neat tower. You ask everyone; no one built it. And nobody in the whole world knows a way a tower like that could happen on its own. Two stories are left: maybe somebody built it and meant to, or maybe blocks can stack that way by themselves in some way nobody has found.

Here is the surprise. You can look at that tower all day, very very close, and looking will never tell you which story is true. Not because you missed a clue. Not because your eyes are too small. Bring the best machine in the world — still the same. Looking and measuring just are not the kind of tool that can answer this. That is how measuring works, always.

So if someone says "I looked, so now I know somebody meant it" — they said more than looking can say. And if someone says "I looked, so now I know nobody meant it" — they also said more than looking can say. Same for both.

How sure are we? Not a thing we found — a bet we chose. It shows nothing is true, not even a little. And we will tell you what would make us give it up.

**What would prove this wrong:** Someone finds one special thing to look at that comes out one way if somebody meant it and a different way if it just happened. That would not tell us who made the tower — but it would show our bet wrong, because looking COULD tell the two stories apart after all.

**What would make us surer:** This one probably stays a bet, because it says what looking itself cannot do. A careful proof, inside a pretend world, that no measure could ever tell the two apart would move its heart to "proved about the pretend world."

## No measuring can tell you what is good to do. You choose what you care about, and then you keep your choice.

*a bet we choose to make — not evidence*

You can measure lots of things: how tall you are, how heavy a rock is, how many blocks in your tower. But measuring can never tell you what you SHOULD care about.

Here is what we CAN show. Think of a big block tower with one small, wobbly block near the bottom. Take good care of that wobbly block, and the whole tower keeps standing. And this is true for lots of things, not just towers: caring for the weakest part is also what keeps the whole from falling. That is a good REASON to choose caring. But a reason is not a proof. It argues FOR the choice; it never PROVES the choice. So we choose it, out in the open, like a promise, and we wrote it on a page anyone can read. The page has a name: Axiomology.

How sure are we? Not something we found by looking at the world. A bet we picked on purpose. We say out loud what would make us give it up.

**What would prove this wrong:** Someone shows one of two things: our promise fights with itself, like a rule that says "do this" and "do not do this" at once; or keeping the promise is the very thing that makes something fall apart sooner, while a thing that breaks the promise lasts longer.

**What would make us surer:** This probably stays a bet too, same reason. A computer showing, inside a set of rules, that you cannot get a "should" without starting from a "should," would move its heart to "proved about the rules."

## We do not know yet: the world's deepest rule-book has no page for the Logos yet.

*we do not know yet — a job still waiting to be done*

Some scientists wrote a very deep book about the tiny world — tinier than dust, where things act strange. Inside it, two stories meet: one starts yesterday and walks forward, one starts tomorrow and walks backward, and they meet in the middle. But that book is built one way only — it looks at things two at a time.

The Logos, our friend, is the together-part of the world. Some of it you can see two at a time. But some only shows when you look at a whole group at once. If the Logos is truly part of the world's deepest rules, that deep book needs a new page — one that holds the whole-group kind. Nobody has written that page yet — not the scientists who made the book, not anyone, and not yet us.

Up above, we already showed the whole-group idea works for everyday things, like coins and blocks. This is the very same question, but down in the tiny strange world. It is real, open work, ours to try, and none of our other ideas lean on how it turns out.

How sure are we? Nobody knows yet. A job still waiting. If this block stays missing, our tower still stands.

**What would prove this wrong:** Someone shows it is already done — a finished account, in that deep book, of whole-group structure with a piece you can measure — making "nobody has done it" false.

**What would make us surer:** Do the work: say what the whole-only part of the two-way tiny-world picture is, write it down carefully, and work out one example. That turns "we do not know" into a real answer.

# Bets we lost (and we keep them)

These are guesses we made and then tested — and the test beat them. We do not erase them. We keep each one right here, with the exact thing that showed us we were wrong, because a page that only shows its wins is not being honest, and because you learn the most from the bets that lost.

## Lost: we said no move on one part alone can raise the togetherness meter. A two-line trick raised it.

*a bet we lost*

We bet that our first counting-tool could never be tricked: no renaming, no shuffling, no move on one part alone could make it read higher.

Here is what beat it. Take a part that can be minus-one, zero, or one, and a second part that is just the first one times itself. Our tool reads exactly zero. Now swap only the labels "zero" and "one" on the first part — an undoable move, touching one part, peeking at nothing. The tool jumps up. Swap back, and it drops to zero again. The move made nothing real — the two parts were deeply tied the whole time — but our tool was reading the NAMES we gave the numbers, and naming is a choice. We had tied a true law to a tool that does not keep it.

What we learned: the true book lives UNDER the names, and any one tool is only a guess at it. That lesson became the measured "true books" claim above. We keep this loss so the lesson stays honest.

## Lost: we said two things with the same weight-and-warmth everywhere must bend space the same way. The world itself says no.

*a bet we lost*

We bet that gravity reads only "how much," so two things with exactly the same weight-and-warmth at every point must dent space in exactly the same way.

What beat it: a passing ripple in empty space. There is a kind of empty space — a gravity wave rolling through — that has zero weight-and-warmth at every single point, just like plain nothing, and yet space there is still bent, and a real machine can feel the difference. Gravity's rule fixes only PART of the bending from the stuff at each point; the rest is free and can travel, like a wave with no water. Real machines have felt these waves in empty space and even read the sizes of the faraway things that made them. So gravity, as a field spreading out, DOES carry arrangement — outward, after the fact.

What survives is the honest, narrower claim above: at the moment gravity couples to a thing, it prices "how much," never "what kind." We keep this loss to mark exactly where the old, too-big words failed.

## Lost: we read π as the signature of coming-back-around. Its own count found only grammar.

*a bet we lost*

We bet that π (say "pie," about 3.14) is the number of coming-back-around — a signature of return, stamped across all of physics.

What beat it: the bet carried its own test. Take a big physics book and sort every place π shows up into three bins — removable by how we measure turning; forced by round, smooth shapes; or NEITHER. We wrote the rule first, then sorted all 231 places π appears. Every one fell into the first two bins. Each π is either the way we choose to measure turning (radians, one whole turn as 2 π, the tools for finding what repeats) or forced by smooth round geometry (circles, balls, the smooth adding-ups of heat and light). The third bin — a π that is neither — came up empty. Even the hardest cases, the π's that look like fixed numbers hiding in laws about countable things, turned out to be smooth adding-ups whose exact countable versions have no π at all.

So π is grammar, not habit — which our "laws are habits" bet had to admit, and which dented its π-fingerprint hint. We keep this loss with its whole count.

## Lost: we bet that upkeep in smooth time is unavoidably an e-shaped curve. Its own test beat it.

*a bet we lost*

We bet that the number e (about 2.72) is the signature of upkeep — that decay-and-payment, written in smooth time, cannot be written without it.

What beat it: the same kind of three-bin test, on the same big physics book, rule written first. Every appearance of e was sorted, the hard cases settled by exact figuring. The "neither" bin came up empty. Every e was a choice of how to describe: wiggles rebase to plain waves, half-lives to a simple ratio, chancy counts to exact whole-number counts, a famous shortcut to a plain finite sum. The e is in our smooth-time idealisings, not in upkeep itself.

π and e were twins — each read as a signature written into the world, e for upkeep and π for return. Each faced the very same test; each failed the same way. Both are now the notation of how we idealise, not signatures in the world. We keep both losses side by side.

## Lost: we said no time-symmetric tiny-world math yet carries more-than-two-party structure. It has existed since 2009.

*a bet we lost*

We bet that the two-way tiny-world picture — one story from the past meeting one from the future — had no published grown-up version carrying whole-group structure, and that building one was open work.

What beat it: the version already exists. Some scientists built "multiple-time states" back in 2009. They are two-way in time, carry both past and future edges, fold back into the ordinary two-way picture at the two ends, and carry across-many-times togetherness that is not just a two-party thing. Our sentence was simply false when we wrote it: the work we called "future" had been published seventeen years earlier.

What is actually still open — connecting that math to our reading — is the "we do not know yet" claim above. We keep this loss to mark that we once mistook a done thing for an undone one.

## The rules we follow

These are the rules we make ourselves follow. A computer checks some of them every single time we change our work. People must keep all the others.

Say your plan first. (People keep this one.) Before you peek at the answer, write down two things: every step of how you will look — the whole plan, exactly, with no wiggle room — and what each answer would mean if you saw it. Why first? Because after you have seen the answer, you cannot pick your way of looking fairly anymore.

Say how your idea could lose. (People keep this one.) Before you believe an idea, say out loud: "If I see this, my idea is wrong." An idea that can never lose is not really saying anything about the world.

One idea, one fall. (People keep this one.) Every idea gets its own way to be wrong. Think of blocks. Each block should be able to fall down by itself. If one bump knocks down all the blocks at once, then the blocks were glued into one lump. Glued blocks were never really tested one by one.

Make a fair pretend pile. (People keep this one.) Sometimes you think you found something real in your stuff. To check, make a pretend pile with nothing special hiding in it, and compare it to your real pile. But the pretend pile must be made the same way as the real one. If your real stuff is things you count, like coins in cups, the pretend stuff must be counted coins too. A pretend pile made the wrong way fools people more often than any other mistake.

Tell how many were the same, out of all of them. (People keep this one.) Some ways of checking first put everything in a line, smallest to biggest, then look. But what if many things are exactly the same, like many empty cups? The line still has to order them, so it makes up an order that was never real. That is why you must always tell how many were the same out of the whole pile. And when a bigger share is the same, the made-up part gets bigger right along with it.

Take away the tiny extra. (People keep this one.) When you check how much things go together, your pile always has an end — no pile goes on forever — and just because the pile has an end, the tool always shows a tiny bit, even when there is truly nothing there. So mix your stuff all up, so anything real gets broken, and measure the mixed-up stuff. The tiny bit you still see is the extra. Take it away from your real answer.

A leftover is not a yes. (People keep this one.) Maybe there is a little piece your idea cannot explain. That leftover does not show your idea is right. An idea gets help only one way: it says "this will happen" before it happens — and then it really happens.

Tell it when you lose. (People keep this one.) Sometimes a test shows your own favorite idea is wrong. Then you say so, just as loud and clear as if you had won. And you keep the dead idea in the book, and write next to it: this one died. (The whole "bets we lost" part up above is this rule, in action.)

Seeing nothing is not the same as nothing being there. (People keep this one.) Some tools are blind to some things. If a blind tool says "nothing here," that does not mean nothing is there. It only means you did not look with a tool that could see it.

No "sorry" holes. (The computer checks this one.) A computer checks some of our proofs, step by step. If we skip a step, we must write a special word there: "sorry." A "sorry" is a hole, and no holes are allowed on the page we show people. If a part is not finished, we say "this part is still open." We never quietly pretend it is done.

Know what the proof stands on. (The computer checks this one.) Every proof stands on starting ideas, the way a tower stands on its bottom blocks. A proof is only as strong as its bottom blocks. So the computer writes down the list of bottom blocks every single time. We do not just try to remember them.
