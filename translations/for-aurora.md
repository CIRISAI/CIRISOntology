Our guess also breaks if people write down the rules of a fair test first, before they look, and then the other story — where the dark stuff moves by itself — fits the same clues better than ours. Not just as well. Better.

## Looking can never tell us: did someone mean to make the tidy thing, or did it just happen all by itself?

*wager*

Pretend you find your blocks stacked in a tall, neat tower. You ask everyone. No one says they built it. And no one in the whole world knows a way a tower like that could happen. So there are two stories left. Maybe somebody built it, and they meant to. Or maybe blocks can stack up that way all by themselves, in some way nobody has found yet.

Here is the surprise. You can look at that tower all day. You can look very, very close. Looking will never tell you which story is true. And it is not because you missed a clue. It is not because your eyes are too small. Bring a magnifying glass. Bring the best machine in the whole world. It is still the same. Looking and measuring are just not the kind of tools that can answer this question. That is how measuring works, always.

So if someone says, "I looked, so now I know somebody meant it" — they said more than looking can say. And if someone says, "I looked, so now I know nobody meant it" — they also said more than looking can say. It is the same for both of them.

How sure are we? This one is not a thing we found out. It is a bet we chose. The bet does not show anything is true — not even a little bit. And we will tell you what would make us give the bet up.

**What would prove this wrong:** Someone could find one special thing to look at — a thing that looks one way when somebody meant it, and a different way when it just happened by itself. Finding that special thing would not yet tell us who made your tower. But it would show our bet was wrong, because looking could tell the two stories apart after all. Then we would give the bet up, and we would say so.

## No measuring can tell you what is good to do. You choose what you care about, and then you keep your choice.

*wager*

You can measure lots of things. How tall you are. How heavy a rock is. How many blocks are in your tower. But measuring can never tell you what you should care about.

Here is what we CAN show. Think of a big block tower. One block near the bottom is small and wobbly. If you take good care of that wobbly block, the whole tower keeps standing. And this is not only about towers. It is like this for lots and lots of things. Taking care of the weakest part is also what keeps the whole thing from falling down.

That is a good reason to choose caring. But a reason is not a proof. This reason does not prove you must care. And measuring cannot prove it either. So you choose it, like choosing a promise. We chose it. We wrote our choice on a page anyone can go and read. The page has its own name. Its name is Axiomology. That is just the name of our page, like the name of a book.

How sure are we? This part is not something we found out by looking at the world. It is a bet we picked on purpose. A bet is not the same as showing something is true. And we say out loud what would make us give the bet up.

**What would prove this wrong:** We would give up our bet if someone showed one of two things. One: our promise fights with itself, like a rule that says "do this" and "do not do this" at the same time. Two: keeping the promise is the very thing that makes something fall apart sooner — it breaks because it keeps the promise, and a thing that does not keep the promise lasts longer.

## We do not know yet: the world's deepest rule book has no page for the Logos yet.

*open*

Some scientists before us wrote a very deep book about how the world works. Inside it, two stories meet. One story starts yesterday and walks forward. One story starts tomorrow and walks backward. They meet in the middle. But that book is built one way only: it looks at things two at a time, like friends holding hands, two by two.

The Logos is a name, like the name of a new friend. It is our name for the together-part of the world — the way many things can share one way of going. Some of that togetherness you can see two at a time. But some of it only shows when you look at the whole group at once, like a game that needs everybody playing.

If the Logos is truly part of the world's deepest rules, then that deep book needs a new page. The new page must hold the whole-group kind. Nobody has written that page yet.

Up above on this page, we already showed the whole-group idea works for everyday things, like counting blocks and coins. That part is done. This question is the very same question, but for the world of very tiny things — tinier than dust — where things act strange.

How sure are we? Nobody knows the answer yet — not us, not anyone. It is a job still waiting to be done. And none of our other ideas sit on top of this one. If this block stays missing, our tower still stands.

**What would prove this wrong:** Someday someone might show, so surely that nobody can argue, this: no matter who tries, and no matter how they try, no new page can ever be written for that book that holds the whole-group kind — not this page, not any page, not ever. If that happens, the answer here is no. But either way — yes or no — all our other ideas up above stay standing.

## The rules we follow

These are the rules we make ourselves follow. A computer checks some of them every single time we change our work. People must keep all the others.

Say your plan first. (People keep this one.) Before you peek at the answer, write down two things: every step of how you will look — the whole plan, exactly, with no wiggle room — and what each answer would mean if you saw it. Why first? Because after you have seen the answer, you cannot pick your way of looking fairly anymore.

Say how your idea could lose. (People keep this one.) Before you believe an idea, say out loud: "If I see this, my idea is wrong." An idea that can never lose is not really saying anything about the world.

One idea, one fall. (People keep this one.) Every idea gets its own way to be wrong. Think of blocks. Each block should be able to fall down by itself. If one bump knocks down all the blocks at once, then the blocks were glued into one lump. Glued blocks were never really tested one by one.

Make a fair pretend pile. (People keep this one.) Sometimes you think you found something real in your stuff. To check, make a pretend pile with nothing special hiding in it, and compare it to your real pile. But the pretend pile must be made the same way as the real one. If your real stuff is things you count, like coins in cups, the pretend stuff must be counted coins too. A pretend pile made the wrong way fools people more often than any other mistake. It is the way people trick themselves most.

Tell how many were the same, out of all of them. (People keep this one.) Some ways of checking first put everything in a line, from smallest to biggest, and only then look. But what if many things are exactly the same? Like many empty cups — every cup holds nothing, so they are all the same. The line still has to put them in some order. So it makes up an order that was never real. That is why you must always tell how many were the same out of the whole pile — like saying "seven of my ten cups were empty." And here is the rule: when a bigger share of the pile is the same, the made-up part gets bigger too. It grows right along with it.

Take away the tiny extra. (People keep this one.) Here is a strange thing. When you check how much things go together, your pile of stuff always has an end — no pile goes on forever. And just because the pile has an end, the tool always shows a tiny little bit — even when there is truly nothing there. So do this. Mix your stuff all up, so anything real in it gets broken. Measure the mixed-up stuff. The tiny bit you still see is the extra. Take that tiny bit away from your real answer.

A leftover is not a yes. (People keep this one.) Maybe there is a little piece your idea cannot explain. That leftover piece does not show your idea is right. An idea gets help only one way: it says "this will happen" before it happens — and then it really happens.

Tell it when you lose. (People keep this one.) Sometimes a test shows that your own favorite idea is wrong. Then you must say so, just as loud and just as clear as if you had won. And you keep the dead idea in the book. You write next to it: this one died.

Seeing nothing is not the same as nothing being there. (People keep this one.) Some tools are blind to some things. If a blind tool says "nothing here," that does not mean nothing is there. It only means you did not look with a tool that could see it.

No "sorry" holes. (The computer checks this one.) A computer checks some of our proofs, step by step. If we skip a step, we must write a special word there. The word is "sorry." A "sorry" is a hole. No holes are allowed on the page we show people. If a part is not finished, we say "this part is still open." We never quietly pretend it is done.

Know what the proof stands on. (The computer checks this one.) Every proof stands on starting ideas, the way a tower stands on its bottom blocks. A proof is only as strong as its bottom blocks. So the computer writes down the list of bottom blocks every single time. We do not just try to remember them.
